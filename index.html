About

Golden Drive

A very inclusive archive of useful resources for medical students of Sultan Qaboos University hosted on Google Drive. The drive was founded by Abdulrahman Al-Hadhrami from the 2018 batch. Resources scattered across multiple drives were collected and combined into one single drive. Resources include lectures, tutorial solutions, practical notes, lecture summaries, Anki flashcards, past papers and extra questions, book and video libraries, and more.

The Website

This website is a fancy browser for the content of the Golden Drive on Google Drive. It was developed by Jihad Al-Yahamadi from the 2018 batch. Whatever files get uploaded, changed, or deleted from the drive, this website will update its content accordingly, as both are directly connected.

Disclaimer

The majority of the content in this drive were made by other students and is just being organised here. The content may be incorrect or outdated. If you would like to report anything incorrect, please contact any of us and submit your report for us to see.

Study Tips

Phase 1

  • This will be the easiest phase you will take. You will take introductory courses in medicine and you will take 3 university requirements and 3 university electives.
  • Do not sleep on the electives and university requirements. Although they do not count towards the focused GPA, they count towards your cumulative GPA.

  • The notes you write from Dr. Faizah's explanation are the most important. Make sure to write everything important she says.
  • Whenever you feel that you got an unfair mark in an exam or test, be sure to discuss it with your instructor as they tend to make a lot of mistakes while marking. From our experiences, we found that they messed up in the marking of multiple questions on an ICA, and this happened many times on quizzes too.
  • Never hesitate to ask any question to Dr. Faizah.

  • Focus on the medical terminology quizzes, as they probably require the most of your effort in this course, and they are the easiest to guarantee a full mark in.
  • Make sure to do the medical terminology practice quizzes on Moodle many times. A huge amount of those questions do come in the real quizzes.
  • It is recommended to make flashcards using Anki to help you memorise the medical terminology better.
  • Do not procrastinate on the presentations; start as early as possible.

  • Make sure you practise using the softwares and practical concepts taught. The practicals contribute the most towards your final grade.
  • When given assignments, make sure you follow the instructions given well so that you do not lose unnecessary marks on silly mistakes. This happened a lot in our batch.
  • If you suspect that there are multiple correct answers to a question, report it to Dr. Ken and explain to him why multiple answers are correct. He will likely solve the problem and correct everyone's grades if your reasoning is sound. This also happened a couple of times in our batch.

  • Resources: Histology Resources, Gross Anatomy Resources
  • It is highly recommended that you use Anki to help you memorise better as this course is heavily dependent on memorisation. You can use Husain Al-Aswami's deck in the drive.
  • Take notes from Dr. Hussein Abdullatif's lectures; he tends to say stuff that comes in the exams but is not in the lectures.
  • Dr. Rao focuses a lot on blood/lymphatic/nerve supply in his exam questions, so be sure to memorise them well.
  • Come up with as many mnemonics as you can to help you memorise.

  • Resources: Physiology Resources
  • Be sure to understand the immune system very well because in the final exam they tend to get annoying questions from that system.
  • Take notes from Dr. Hussain Saker's lectures; he tends to say stuff that comes in the exams but is not in the lectures.

  • Resources: Enzymology Resources
  • The book given is more detailed than what is required for you to learn. You do not have to know a lot of those things, so if you come across something obscure, leave it until the time of the tutorial, then ask the instructor about it.
  • When studying metabolic pathways, only focus on the rate-limiting steps, the enzymes involved in those steps, and what inhibits/stimulates them. The other steps are less important.

  • Resources: Cell Physiology Resources, Pharmacology Resources
  • Study very well for the quizzes because they carry more weight than they should (10 total questions for 10% of your grade).
  • Make sure you understand definitions well in pharmacology because they tend to make very tricky questions in the exams.
  • It is highly recommended to use Anki to memorise the third block in pharmacology as it involves a lot of memorisation.

Phase 2 Semester 1

  • Guide me project: Phase 2 Semester 1
  • This is either the most or the second most difficult semester in your preclinical studies. This is due to time constraints and your transition to 18 credits a semester.
  • Starting from this semester, there will be way more focus on tutorials and practicals than in phase 1. Many of us did not give them much attention because we were used to them being less important in phase 1. Do not make the same mistake.

  • Guide me project: Response to Infection and Pathobiology
  • Always use SketchyMicro when possible. Almost all microbes are covered.
  • Using Anki is highly recommended for this course. Use the available pre-made Anki decks for microbiology. Husain Al-Aswami's Anki deck covers all sections of microbiology, make sure to use it. You can also use the Anking deck to do cards based on Sketchy.
  • Make sure to be familiar with the distinguishing lab tests for each microbe and to understand what the test is. This is very important in bacteriology.
  • Your virology lectures will be most arguably the worst lectures you will encounter this semester. You can opt to stick with the slides and brute memorize its content. You can go the extra mile and watch the relevant Sketchy videos.
  • For immunology, make sure to watch Pathoma's videos on inflammation (Chapter 2). You can use Kaplan's Lecture Notes on immunology to make sure you have the whole picture of immunology especially when it comes to the types of immune responses.
  • Most pathology lectures are straightforward. However, you can complement them with Pathoma's videos on the relevant topics (Chapter 1).
  • Make sure to be familiar with all practical materials (mostly pictures). You can do yourself a great favour by trying to understand the general pathophysiology of the given cases.
  • Pharmacology is arguably one of the toughest parts of RIP due to the large amount of content you need to go through. Make sure to make mnemonics, surprisingly, the best ones are the inappropriate ones. You can opt to complement your lectures with Sketchy Pharm videos.

  • Guide me project: Structure-Function Relationship
  • Use Anki to help you memorise. This course is heavily dependent on memorisation.
  • The anatomy lectures are just like your general anatomy from phase 1. This course pays more attention to blood and nerve supply of each region. The upper and lower limbs are not covered.
  • The physiology lectures are also similar to your introduction to physiology from phase 1. They cover more details.
  • For surface anatomy, make sure you memorise the levels/landmarks well (e.g., the vertebral level/rib at which a structure is located); those are very important.

    • Guide me project: Hospital & Community Attachment
    • Use the already available summaries to help you study the content.
    • For biostatistics, try to practice as often as possible. Do not just look at the questions, do them yourself.
    • For SPSS, just go through all of Dr. Rizvi's lectures and the accompanying questions/exercises.
    • Start working on the essay immediately by gathering references and giving yourself deadlines to complete each section of the essay. You have a few weeks from the start of the semester before you become busy with in-course assessments, so use those to do as much work as you can.
    • Ask your friends to check your essay for mistakes and get their insights on it.

  • Guide me project: Molecular Biology and Genetics
  • AK lectures is a great resource for molecular biology and general neoplasia. Shomu's biology YouTube channel is very handy especially in the last few lectures where you are required to apply your knowledge on practical questions.
  • Way too many final exam questions came from the very last few practicals and accompanying lectures, so focus on them.

    • Guide me project: Growth & Development.
    • This course is very easy to neglect, so try to avoid cramming lectures the night before the exam. You might want to do 2 lectures once every couple of weeks to avoid falling behind. Most students survive this course by studying only on the weekend before the exam
    • Osmosis is a great external resource for understanding embryology.
    • Take notes from Dr. Hussein Abdullatif's lectures; he tends to say stuff that comes in the exams but is not in the lectures.

    Phase 2 Semester 2

    • Guide me project: Phase 2 Semester 2
    • This semester is less intense compared to the first semester in phase 2.
    • There is even more focus towards tutorials/practicals in this semester compared to last semester.
    • You will be having more pharmacology lectures than in semester 1, but they are slightly easier to study and memorise.

    • Guide me project: Cardiovascular System
    • Use Anki whenever possible, especially for pharmacology. Sketchy Pharm is really recommended for studying the pharmacology lectures.
    • Even if the instructors tell you that you do not have to memorise ECGs, it is still better to study them, as they will help you in exams.
    • Although you will not take many microbiology lectures, a lot of exam questions came from them.
    • When studying diseases, you do not need to memorise every single symptom as that is a waste of time. Just get the general idea of the symptoms. Usually in exams they give you a case and you are required to figure out what the condition is, so make sure you are able to do that.
    • Osmosis is a good external source for pathology reviews.
    • Boards and Beyond is probably the best external resource for the cardiovascular system in general.

    • Guide me project: Haematopoietic and Immune Systems
    • This course specifically relies way too much of SAQs, so practise them well.
    • Unfortunately, relying solely on lectures will not get you far. You have to rely on external resources to score well.
    • Suggested external resources to rely on: Review of Medical Microbiology and Immunology, Roitt's Essential Immunology, and Kaplan's book on immunology and microbiology.
    • Pathoma is a highly recommend resource for hematology; everything is well-covered there.
    • :)

    • Guide me project: Integrated Module 1
    • Almost everything in this course has or will be covered this semester in other courses, so do not worry too much about them.
    • Osmosis is a very good resource for this course.

    • Guide me project: Respiratory System
    • Use Anki whenever possible, especially for pharmacology. Sketchy Pharm is really recommended for studying the pharmacology lectures.
    • Pathoma is a very good resource for pathology.
    • Osmosis is a good external source for pathology reviews.

    • Guide me project: Medical Informatics 2
    • Frequently Asked Questions: Medical Informatics 2 FAQ
    • When choosing the presentation topic, try to pick one that is presented in the earlier weeks so that you can get it done before you become busy with in-course assessments.
    • For the book, you only need to study the parts of the book Dr. Ken referred to in his lectures.

    • Guide me project: Research Methodology
    • Most of the lectures are the same as the ones you took in Hospital and Community Attachment.
    • When choosing a research topic, look up the supervisor and check whether they have published many studies. This will give you a rough idea of whether your supervisor has experience in research. Also ask your colleagues about the supervisor.
    • Try your best to aim for a publication. This is useful for you in the future.
    • Be in good terms with your supervisor; in later semesters, your supervisor will also evaluate your performance, which will be part of the grading.
    • Look up past research proposals by other students to get a general idea of what you should be doing.
    • Ask your friends to check your research proposal for mistakes and get their insights on it.
